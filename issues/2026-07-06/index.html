01

AI-search monitoring became a real product category — and an agency service

What's happening

On July 3, Profound launched "Aim," an always-on background agent that continuously monitors AI-search signals — brand visibility, prompt trends, citations, sentiment, and competitive benchmarks across ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity — and turns them into prioritized project briefs. The same week, agency Avenue Z announced it had become a Profound Agency Partner, packaging AI-visibility measurement as a client service.

Why it matters

AEO is going through the same maturation SEO went through a decade ago: it's shifting from a content tactic to a monitored channel with tooling, benchmarks, and a reporting cadence. Set it up as a channel now, while it's still early enough to be a head start.

How to act

Treat AI visibility as a tracked channel, not a one-off project. Stand up a monthly check: run a fixed set of 10–15 real buyer questions through ChatGPT, Gemini, and Perplexity, log whether you're cited and how you're described, and turn the gaps into content briefs. It's low-lift, repeatable, and doubles as proof you can show.

Freshness — not ranking — now decides who AI cites

What's happening

Recent AEO data shows ~83% of AI citations on commercial/evaluation queries come from pages updated within the last 12 months (60%+ within six months), and the overlap between Google's organic top-10 and what AI Overviews actually cite has weakened from ~76% in mid-2025 to somewhere between 17% and 54% in early 2026. Google's new AI-search guidance frames AEO/GEO as "still SEO," but the practical mechanic has changed: structure, freshness, and credible sourcing are the three levers that most reliably predict whether a page gets quoted.

Why it matters

A page that ranked #1 for two years can now be invisible inside the AI answer a buyer actually reads. Update cadence is now a ranking factor for citations.

How to act

Add a standing refresh pass to your highest-intent pages: restate the core answer in the first 100 words, add a dated stat, and stamp the update. Put it on a monthly cadence for cornerstone articles and product/spec pages — with dated, extractable facts — so they read as current authoritative sources and stay inside the citation window instead of aging out of the answer.

03

Agentic AI just got cheaper — and so did the work

What's happening

Anthropic released Claude Sonnet 5 on June 30 and made it the default for all Free and Pro users starting July 1. Pitched as its most agentic mid-size model, Sonnet 5 performs near flagship Opus on many tasks, at introductory pricing (through Aug 31) below the prior Sonnet. In parallel, martech vendors are shipping "background agents" (Profound Aim, Typeface's ad/video agents) that run multi-step work with a human approving at the end.

Why it matters

The marginal cost of agentic execution — drafting, monitoring, first-pass research — keeps dropping. The differentiator is no longer access to the model; it's having the workflows and review process to use it reliably.

How to act

Cheaper models make "draft → human review" workflows economical to run at scale. Pick one repeatable task — content refresh, first-pass research, the AI-visibility check above — and stand one up. The cost of trying just dropped; the win is the workflow and the human review around it.

What's happening

A cluster of July-3 deals made the pattern explicit — Zoom agreed to acquire Common Room (a buyer-intelligence platform unifying first-party CRM, product, and engagement data), and the week's analysis argued the AI model itself is commoditizing while the data and decisioning layers beneath it become the real differentiator. BCG data cited alongside it: only ~8% of CMOs run campaigns with multiple autonomous agents, and 42% still use GenAI only to assist with individual tasks.

Why it matters

Agentic systems are only as good as the data they sit on. The winners over the next year won't be the ones who adopt the flashiest agent — they'll be the ones whose first-party data (CRM hygiene, unified contact records, engagement history) is clean enough for agents to act on.

How to act

Before layering any AI onto outreach, run a first-party data-hygiene pass: dedupe accounts, correct firmographics, and consolidate engagement history. It's the unglamorous prerequisite that makes everything downstream — ABM, AI-assisted personalization — actually work, and "get your first-party data agent-ready" is a credible, differentiated offering most skip.

05

LinkedIn now rewards relevance and native text — carousels and external links are penalized

−60%
reach penalty on posts with external links
~5% vs ~65%
feed allocation to company pages vs personal profiles

What's happening

LinkedIn's 2026 distribution overhaul (its "Generative Recommenders" system) shifted reach from follower count to topical relevance. Reported effects: native text posts outperforming carousels for B2B organic reach (one analysis of 147 B2B accounts found native posts averaged ~28% higher reach and ~34% better engagement), a "depth score" now weighting dwell time / comment quality over raw likes, and external links cutting reach by ~60%. Company pages get ~5% of feed allocation versus ~65% for personal profiles.

Why it matters

Consistent topical posting from individual experts now builds the "topical credibility" the algorithm rewards — and the link-in-first-comment / native-content playbook matters more than ever.

How to act

Move LinkedIn to native text posts published from individuals' personal profiles, not the company page; keep external links in the first comment; and anchor every post to a narrow topic lane so the algorithm associates those accounts with the theme. Short, opinionated, story-first posts on a tight topical set beat carousels and reach-chasing.

06

Hard email enforcement at Gmail / Yahoo / Microsoft threatens cold-outreach programs

0.10%
spam-complaint ceiling for bulk senders
SPF · DKIM · DMARC
all now required — failures are permanently rejected, not deferred

What's happening

As of late 2025 and enforced through 2026, Gmail, Yahoo, and Microsoft moved from temporary deferrals to permanent rejection for mail that fails authentication. SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are all now required (a syntactically clean SPF, not just any SPF), one-click unsubscribe (RFC 8058) is mandatory for bulk senders, and the spam-complaint ceiling is ~0.10%. Microsoft's enforcement hit B2B hardest because it hosts most corporate inboxes.

Why it matters

Any program that leans on cold or bulk email is now one authentication gap away from silent, permanent rejection — not the spam folder, but never delivered.

How to act

Audit sending-domain authentication before scaling any cold or bulk email: confirm a clean SPF plus DKIM and DMARC, keep one-click unsubscribe in place, warm a dedicated sending subdomain to protect the primary domain's reputation, and watch the complaint rate against the 0.10% ceiling. Deliverability, not copy, is the first bottleneck — and B2B outreach into Microsoft-hosted inboxes is the most exposed. [Flag: confirm sending-domain setup before scaling.]

07

Your buyers go to AI first — and speed-to-lead decides the rest

What's happening

Across high-consideration, local service categories — home services, healthcare, financial and legal — buyers increasingly open with an AI tool, asking the comparison and trust questions before they ever reach your site. And in those same categories, most inbound inquiries still never get a fast human reply: lead-response research consistently finds the majority of web inquiries go unanswered within a day, and many never answered at all. The pattern travels to any considered purchase where a real person is comparing options under pressure.

Why it matters

Two things now decide these categories: whether AI tools surface and trust you, and whether a real human answers fast. It's easy to pour budget into ad creative while a buyer who already raised their hand waits a day for a reply — or never hears back at all.

How to act

For any local, high-consideration service business, run the same two-part play: make sure the pages AI tools read to answer buyer questions ("best [category] near [area]," "cost of X," "X vs. Y") are current, specific, and carry trust signals — accreditations, reviews, transparent pricing — then audit inbound response time. When most inquiries go unanswered within 24 hours, a faster human reply often moves more deals than any new ad spend. It's a repeatable "AI-visibility + speed-to-lead audit" pattern.
