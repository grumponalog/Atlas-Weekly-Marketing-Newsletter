03

The Source Gets the Click

What actually moved in marketing the week of May 25 - AI search turned into a real referral channel, a person's post out-earned every ad, and being the cited source started paying. And how to act on each.

01

The core update lands mid-rollout - every page is on the table

What's happening

Google released the May 2026 core update on May 21 with a rollout up to two weeks - still finishing into early June, across every industry and language. Pages flagged weak on usefulness, originality, author trust, or intent match are the ones getting reshuffled; arriving two days after Google's AI-search keynote, many read it as an AI-era trust calibration.

Why it matters

Core updates reweight everything that already exists - thin pages, unclear authorship, or stale technical content can drop without warning, and recovery needs real content changes, not quick fixes.

How to act

Once the rollout settles, pull rankings on your highest-value pages. Where they slip, the fix is usually authorship and proof: add a named, credentialed author, a visible “last reviewed” date, and real supporting prose to anything thin. Undated, unsigned pages are the most exposed.

02

AI Overviews learns who you follow - and rewards it

What's happening

On May 27 Google widened Preferred Sources: any site publishing fresh content is eligible to be cited in AI Overviews and AI Mode, and users see a “Subscribed” label when an answer pulls from something they follow - which Google says makes them significantly more likely to click. It builds on inline citations, hover previews, and a new “Expert Advice” block pulling first-hand perspectives from forums, social, and reviews.

Why it matters

The unit of competition is no longer “the page that ranks” - it's “the source AI cites.” And followership just became a flywheel: email, LinkedIn, and newsletter subscribers now earn you visible credit inside the answer.

How to act

Two moves: get your audience subscribed and following now, so you earn the “Subscribed” label when they ask - lead your signup with the newsletter, not just the offer. And feed the “Expert Advice” block by having real experts answer substantively in the two or three communities your buyers use.

03

ChatGPT's branded links stick - referrals up ~60% and holding

~60%
sustained lift in ChatGPT referral traffic
7.1%
ChatGPT referral conversion - second only to paid search

What's happening

The May 7 branded-link change - brand names now clickable inside answers, not just cited at the bottom - isn't reverting. The share of answers carrying a brand link jumped from ~0.4% to ~6.2%, about 14x, and months on the referral lift holds around 60%, converting at 7.1%.

Why it matters

For a year, “AI search is a traffic sink” was true. It's now provably wrong for branded queries - ChatGPT behaves like a referral source. If your brand gets mentioned but not linked, you're leaving real clicks on the table.

How to act

Make your brand easy to attach a link to: keep GPTBot un-blocked in robots.txt and your brand language consistent across your site, LinkedIn, and anywhere you're quoted. Then test monthly - ask the assistant “what is [brand]?” and check for the inline link; where it's missing, that's the gap to close.

04

A real person's post is quietly outperforming every ad

6.9x
Thought Leader Ad CTR vs. every other format (4.65% vs 0.68%)
$0.51 vs $2.42
cost per click, Thought Leader Ads vs. standard

What's happening

2026 LinkedIn benchmarks landed with a wider gap than expected: Thought Leader Ads (sponsoring a real person's post) average 4.65% CTR versus 0.68% for every other format - a 6.9x lift - at $0.51 CPC versus $2.42, with CPL 40-50% lower. The algorithm backs it: relevance-based distribution, a ~60% link penalty, and personal profiles at 8x the engagement of company pages.

Why it matters

The cost of a B2B click through a real human's profile is now roughly a fifth of one through a company page. For any service brand with a credible practitioner voice, it's the biggest paid arbitrage of 2026.

How to act

Put a modest four-week Thought Leader Ad behind a named leader's best post on a specific idea, tightly targeted to your buyer. Benchmark to ~4% CTR; if it hits, run two a month with rotating leaders. Thought Leader Ads reward a strong point of view, not “5 tips” filler.

05

AI agents go from demo to shipping - and the winners are vertical

What's happening

A wave of agent platforms shipped real utility in two weeks - Madhive for local ad placement, Alteryx's Agent Studio for autonomous data workflows, Attentive's agentic messaging, Figma's multi-agent canvas. Gartner projects 40% of enterprise apps will ship task-specific agents by end of 2026, up from under 5% a year ago.

Why it matters

The AI story shifted from generation (copy, images) to orchestration (agents doing multi-step work). The agents that win aren't horizontal “do everything” tools - they're vertical, tied to a specific workflow. That's a positioning opening.

How to act

Pick a lane, not a platform: position around the specific workflow you own, and pair clients with the right vertical agent instead of rebuilding automations from scratch. A one-page “agent map” matching the new platforms to your buyers' real use cases makes a timely lead magnet.

06

Deliverability enforcement is biting - an infrastructure problem in disguise

22-34%
spam-folder rate for non-compliant senders, up from 5-10%
~30%
of bulk senders still partially non-compliant

What's happening

Gmail, Yahoo, and Microsoft moved from warning to quietly routing you to spam. The 2024 bulk-sender rules (SPF + DKIM + DMARC, one-click unsubscribe, a low complaint rate) are now enforced hard - non-compliant senders see spam-folder delivery jump from 5-10% to 22-34%, and ~30% are still partially non-compliant. Trip the complaint threshold and you're ineligible for remediation until it stays low for seven straight days.

Why it matters

Deliverability is now an infrastructure problem disguised as a marketing one. For anyone running cold outreach or large nurtures, one unaudited DKIM record or a soft DMARC policy is a measurable revenue leak.

How to act

Audit the plumbing: SPF, DKIM, and DMARC published and aligned; DMARC at p=quarantine minimum; one-click unsubscribe in every template; send from a warmed subdomain, not your root domain. It's a one-day job that decides whether the campaign you already built ever reaches an inbox.
