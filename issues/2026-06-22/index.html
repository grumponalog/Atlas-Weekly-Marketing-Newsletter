Train Now, Cited Later

What actually moved in marketing the week of June 22-29 - AEO became a measurable channel and a cadence game, and most of what you publish now trains next quarter's answers. And how to act on each.

01

An AEO layer enters the stack - and it's train-now, cite-later

~52% vs ~10%
AI crawler traffic for training vs. live search

What's happening

The martech stack sprouted a dedicated answer-engine layer - Optimizely shipped an AEO platform with “Agent Visibility Analytics” to show how AI engines read and cite you. Cloudflare's June data shows ~51.8% of AI crawler traffic is for model training and only ~10% for live AI search.

Why it matters

AI visibility is shifting from guesswork to a measurable channel - and the training-vs-live split reframes publishing as a long game: what you ship this quarter is what the models repeat next quarter.

How to act

Publish definitive, entity-clean reference pages for the things you want to own - answer-first, so engines ingest you as the canonical source. Treat it as an investment that compounds; and if you sell services, an “AI visibility / AEO audit” is now a real, buyable engagement.

02

AI research surfaces become a real discovery channel

What's happening

In one week, OpenAI and Perplexity both pushed into guided buying: ChatGPT's “shopping research” asks clarifying questions, pulls price, spec, and review data, and builds a tailored buyer's guide; Perplexity launched a free conversational shopping experience that remembers preferences across a session.

Why it matters

Buyers - and families - increasingly start product and vendor research inside an AI assistant, not Google. If your specs, reviews, and comparison content aren't legible to these tools, you're invisible in the new first step of the journey.

How to act

Structure your product and comparison content as clean, comparable data - specs, materials, options, compliance, transparent pricing signals, current third-party reviews - so when someone asks an assistant to “compare the best X for Y,” your data is what gets pulled. Make “where to buy” machine-readable too.

03

The nonprofit AI gap is quantified - a positioning gift

92% → 7%
use AI vs. see major mission impact
81%
use AI individually, with no shared workflow

What's happening

A Virtuous + Fundraising.AI report found 92% of nonprofits use AI but only 7% report major mission-capability gains, and 81% use it individually with no shared workflows. Separately, the OpenAI Foundation opened a $50M People-First AI Fund for nonprofits (applications June 15-July 15).

Why it matters

A whole sector adopted the tools; almost none built the systems. That gap is the wedge for anyone selling outcomes - and it's now backed by third-party data you can cite instead of assert.

How to act

Lead with the line: “everyone has the tools; almost no one has the system.” Build your problem statement around the cited stat (attribute it) and sell the system-and-strategy layer over another point tool. A short “systems vs. tools” benchmark makes a strong lead magnet.

Sources  NonProfit PRO - adoption hits 92%, 7% see impact · OpenAI Foundation - People-First AI Fund

04

Google: AEO is “still SEO,” and cadence decides citations

~3.2x
more likely to appear in AI Overviews with FAQPage schema

What's happening

Google's new guidance frames AEO and GEO as part of SEO, not a separate discipline. FAQ rich results left the SERP (May 7) but Google still reads FAQPage schema - pages using it are ~3.2x more likely to appear in AI Overviews. Citation studies: 83% of AI citations go to pages updated in the last year, and pages not refreshed quarterly are ~3x more likely to lose citations.

Why it matters

The winning move isn't a tool - it's an operating rhythm: keep schema in place, refresh high-value pages quarterly, and write answer-first with a quote and a stat (which lift visibility ~41% and ~30%).

How to act

Stand up a quarterly refresh sprint on your highest-intent pages: a dated “last updated,” an answer-first summary, one credible quote, one stat, and FAQPage schema. It's the cheapest, highest-leverage move here - and it compounds.

05

LinkedIn re-weights B2B: people, comments, saves - not pages

~65% vs ~5%
feed allocation to personal profiles vs. company pages
~28%
more reach for native text vs. carousels

What's happening

Late-May and June LinkedIn changes reshaped B2B reach: the share button carries far less weight, off-platform links see reduced distribution, and the dominant signals are comment quality (length, OP replies, threads) and save rate. In a 147-account analysis, native text posts averaged ~28% more reach and ~34% better engagement than carousels; personal profiles get ~65% of feed versus ~5% for pages.

Why it matters

Reach is moving decisively toward people, conversation, and on-platform value. Anyone still posting carousels-with-links from a company page is paying a reach tax.

How to act

Move thought leadership to named people's profiles as native text that ends with a real question to spark peer-to-peer comments; put any link in the first comment. Repurpose your next article as a native breakdown, not a carousel, and measure comments and saves, not impressions.

06

Email enforcement tightened - and it hits B2B hardest

~0.10%
spam-complaint tolerance for bulk senders
Microsoft
hardest-hit - it hosts most corporate inboxes

What's happening

Gmail, Yahoo, and Microsoft now reject mail lacking full authentication. SPF, DKIM, and DMARC are effectively required, one-click unsubscribe (RFC 8058) is mandatory with a two-day honor window, and complaint tolerance sits near 0.10%. Microsoft's enforcement hit B2B senders hardest because it hosts most corporate inboxes.

Why it matters

For cold or bulk outreach this is now a gating technical issue - a misconfigured domain means rejection at the protocol level, not just the spam folder.

How to act

Audit SPF/DKIM/DMARC on the sending domain, wire up one-click unsubscribe, and warm a dedicated subdomain so outreach never torches your primary domain's reputation. Keep the complaint rate under ~0.10% by tightening targeting over volume - especially for B2B into Microsoft-hosted inboxes.

07

Senior-living marketing pivots hard to the family caregiver

~51
median age of the Gen X adult child making the decision

What's happening

Senior Housing News reported operators broadening caregiver-focused marketing - explicitly including male caregivers, not just the “adult daughter” - and leaning on proof-based, real-caregiver storytelling over polished collateral. The decider is typically a ~51-year-old Gen X adult child comparing options online and reading reviews.

Why it matters

The decision is made by an anxious, research-heavy family member, and what moves them is authentic proof - real residents and families, reviews, referral relationships - not brochure language.

How to act

Frame your hero content around real customers telling their own decision stories, not amenity B-roll, and broaden who's on camera beyond the obvious archetype. Build decision-journey content - costs, options, how-to-compare checklists, FAQs - and invest in review-site presence and referral relationships, where trust forms before anyone calls.
