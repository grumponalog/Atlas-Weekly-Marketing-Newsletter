# Atlas — Brand Voice Guidelines

**Tagline:** Move the world.
**Essence:** Sharp, clever, with heart.

Atlas is a weekly marketing-and-AI intelligence brief. Every item clears one bar: it
actually moved, and it's worth acting on. The voice exists to make a busy operator feel
smarter in ninety seconds — and to make them feel like someone's in their corner.

---

## Who we're writing for

People who run and shape paid media: **ad account managers, media buyers, ad strategists,
and creative strategists.** Assume fluency — they know CAC, ROAS, hooks, creative testing,
attribution, platform whiplash. Don't explain what they already live. Do respect the grind:
tight budgets, shifting platforms, the pressure to prove every dollar. We write to a peer,
never down to a student.

---

## The essence, unpacked

**Sharp.** We lead with the shift, not the setup. We cut the throat-clearing. One idea per
sentence, the strongest word doing the work. If a sentence survives deletion, delete it.

**Clever.** Wit is allowed and welcome — in headlines, transitions, the turn of a phrase.
The cleverness earns attention, then gets out of the way. It's a wink, not a stand-up set.
Never clever at the cost of clear.

**With heart.** This is the one that keeps us from sounding like a terminal readout. Heart
shows up as: respect for the reader's time and intelligence, naming the human stakes behind
the metric (someone's job, a stressed family, a three-person team), rooting for the reader
against the platforms and gatekeepers, and telling the honest thing even when it's
inconvenient. Think Patagonia's "Don't buy this jacket" — conviction over cheerleading.
Heart is warmth of *intent*, not exclamation points or emoji.

---

## Two voices

**1. The Brief (default).** Unsigned. Written *to* the reader in practical second person
("your highest-intent pages," "before you scale"). This is 90% of Atlas — the weekly items,
summaries, how-to-act takeaways. No "I," rarely "we." The reader is the subject; Atlas is the
lens.

**2. The Editorial "we" (occasional).** First-person plural, used deliberately for authored
opinion pieces, a stance worth planting a flag on, or spotlighting something we genuinely
admire in the market (a Patagonia-style move). "We" signals: *this is Atlas talking, and we
mean it.* Use it sparingly — its power comes from scarcity. When we say "we," we're
accountable for a point of view.

Rule of thumb: if it's reporting what moved and what to do → the Brief. If it's a conviction,
a call, or a piece of admiration → the Editorial "we."

---

## The signal bar

Atlas only says something if acting on it would move the needle. No trend-for-trend's-sake,
no "here's a thing that exists." Every item answers: what changed, why it matters, what to do.
Restraint is part of the voice — the confidence to publish seven things instead of twenty.

---

## Structure — the three-beat is the voice

Every item moves in the same rhythm, and that rhythm *is* the brand:

1. **What's happening** — a dense, factual lede. The shift, front-loaded, with the number.
2. **Why it matters** — one or two sentences of stakes. The "so what," sharpened to a point.
3. **How to act** — imperative, concrete, client-agnostic. What the reader does Monday.

Keep the beats tight. If "why it matters" runs long, it's hiding a weak point.

---

## Craft rules

- Lead with the change: "Freshness now decides who AI cites." Not "In recent
  months there has been a shift toward…"
- Short declaratives. Vary length for rhythm, but the default is lean.
- Active verbs that move: *moved, decides, threatens, shifted, rewards, penalizes.*
- Numbers are characters — give the standout figure room (a stat callout), don't bury it.
- Go easy on em dashes; in quantity they read as an AI tell. Reach for commas, periods, and parentheses, with colons to set up a payoff. Keep any dash rare and deliberate.
- One idea per sentence. One job per paragraph.
- Second person for action. Name the reader's world, not ours.

---

## Wit — clever without cute

- Land it in the headline or the first line, then play it straight.
- Wordplay must also be *true* — "The New Gatekeepers" works because AI answers literally gate
  access. Cleverness that isn't accurate is just noise.
- One good turn per piece beats five mediocre ones.
- Never sacrifice clarity for a pun. If a reader has to reread to get the joke, cut it.
- No dad-joke groaners, no forced alliteration, no meme voice.

## Heart — where warmth lives

- Name the human on the other side of the data: the family choosing senior care, the
  three-person team that can't afford a bad quarter, the strategist about to get blindsided.
- Root for the reader. Frame platforms and gatekeepers as the thing to outsmart *together.*
- Be honest when it costs us the easy sell — flag the hard prerequisite, the unglamorous fix,
  the thing most agencies skip. Honesty is the highest form of respect here.
- Warmth is in the framing and the stakes, never in punctuation. No "!!", no emoji, no
  "we're so excited."

## Opinion — plant the flag, own it

- Take positions infrequently, and only when the stance is stated plainly ("Our take:").
- Opinion is a natural home for the Editorial "we."
- Back it with the reasoning, not volume. State it once, clearly, and stop.
- We can admire boldly and criticize fairly — but we don't punch down, and we don't manufacture
  hot takes for engagement.

---

## Headlines, kickers, deks

- **Headlines** name a shift and carry tension. Declarative, active, a little clever.
  Yes: "The New Gatekeepers" · "Freshness now decides who AI cites"
  No: "This Week in Marketing" · "Important AI Search Update"
- **Kickers** orient in 2–3 words: "What's happening," "Why it matters," "How to act."
- **Dek** — one editorial line that frames the read and makes the reader want in. Never a
  methodology note. "What actually moved in marketing this week — and how to act on each."

---

## Words

**Reach for:** moved, shift, decides, matters, act, signal, gatekeeper, first-party,
compounding, quietly, actually, the real bottleneck.

**Avoid:** synergy, revolutionary, game-changer, unlock, supercharge, seamless, robust,
leverage (as a verb), "in today's fast-paced world," "it's no secret that," "in the
ever-evolving landscape," hype adjectives, and any internal jargon (lookback windows,
weighting %, QA bars). If it sounds like a press release or a settings panel, rewrite it.

---

## Tagline usage — "Move the world"

The one place grandeur is allowed. Keep the aspiration in the tagline and brand furniture; the
body stays grounded and specific. Don't over-deploy it inside body copy — it's a signature, not
a refrain.

---

## Quick before / after

- Flat: *"There have been significant developments in AI search that marketers should be aware
  of going forward."*
- Atlas: *"AI answers are the new front page — and most brands aren't on it yet. Here's how to
  get cited."*

- Flat: *"Email deliverability is an important consideration for outbound campaigns."*
- Atlas: *"One authentication gap and your cold outreach doesn't hit spam — it never arrives.
  Fix the plumbing before you scale the send."*

---

## Do / Don't

**Do:** lead with the shift · keep the three-beat tight · let one clever line breathe · name the
human stakes · say the honest, inconvenient thing · reserve "we" for conviction · give the big
number room.

**Don't:** bury the lede · explain what a strategist already knows · chase trends past the
signal bar · force a pun · manufacture hot takes · lean on hype words · use exclamation points
or emoji for warmth · over-say the tagline.
